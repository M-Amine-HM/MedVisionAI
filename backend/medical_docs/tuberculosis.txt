Pulmonary Tuberculosis (TB)
Overview:
Tuberculosis is caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, an acid-fast bacillus. It is transmitted via
airborne droplet nuclei and remains a leading infectious disease cause of death worldwide.
TB is a NOTIFIABLE DISEASE in most countries — mandatory reporting to public health is required.

Primary vs. Reactivation TB:
Primary TB: Initial infection; often hilar lymphadenopathy, middle/lower lobe infiltrates
Reactivation TB: From latent infection; classic upper lobe cavitary disease

Radiological Findings (Chest X-ray):
- Upper lobe infiltrates (posterior segments) — most characteristic
- Cavitation (thick-walled) — pathognomonic in the right clinical context
- Fibrotic scarring, calcified granulomas (old/healed TB)
- Miliary pattern (1-2mm nodules) — disseminated/miliary TB
- Hilar/mediastinal lymphadenopathy (primary TB)
- Pleural effusion (exudative, lymphocyte-predominant)

Symptoms (often insidious onset):
Constitutional (classic triad):
- Chronic productive cough > 2-3 weeks
- Night sweats (drenching)
- Unexplained weight loss (>10% body weight)
Pulmonary:
- Low-grade fever (afternoon pyrexia)
- Haemoptysis (blood-streaked sputum or frank bleeding)
- Fatigue and anorexia
- Pleuritic chest pain

Extrapulmonary TB (15-20% of cases):
- Lymphadenitis (most common extrapulmonary site)
- Pleural TB (exudative effusion)
- Bone/joint TB (Pott's disease — vertebral)
- CNS TB (tuberculous meningitis)
- Genitourinary TB (sterile pyuria, hematuria)

Diagnostic Workup:
1. Sputum AFB smear microscopy (rapid, cheap, low sensitivity)
2. Sputum culture on Lowenstein-Jensen or MGIT (gold standard, 2-8 weeks)
3. GeneXpert MTB/RIF (molecular, 2 hours, detects rifampicin resistance)
4. IGRA (QuantiFERON-TB Gold) or tuberculin skin test (TST/Mantoux) for LTBI
5. Chest X-ray (highly suggestive but not diagnostic alone)
6. Bronchoscopy + BAL if sputum-scarce

Standard First-Line Treatment (Drug-Sensitive TB):
Intensive Phase — 2 months (RIPE regimen):
- Rifampicin (R): 10 mg/kg/day (max 600mg) — bactericidal, prevents resistance
- Isoniazid (I): 5 mg/kg/day (max 300mg) — most potent bactericidal
- Pyrazinamide (P): 25-30 mg/kg/day — kills dormant bacilli
- Ethambutol (E): 15-20 mg/kg/day — prevents resistance

Continuation Phase — 4 months (RI):
- Rifampicin + Isoniazid only
- Total duration: 6 months (9 months for CNS, bone TB)

Drug-Resistant TB:
MDR-TB (resistant to R + I): Bedaquiline-based regimens (6+ months)
XDR-TB (MDR + fluoroquinolone resistance): Specialist center required

Side Effects to Monitor:
- Isoniazid: Peripheral neuropathy (supplement B6/pyridoxine), hepatotoxicity
- Rifampicin: Hepatotoxicity, orange discoloration of secretions, drug interactions (CYP450 inducer)
- Pyrazinamide: Hepatotoxicity, hyperuricemia/gout
- Ethambutol: Optic neuritis (monitor visual acuity monthly)
- LFTs monthly for all patients

Directly Observed Therapy (DOT):
Recommended for all TB patients to ensure adherence and prevent resistance.

Infection Control:
- Respiratory isolation (negative pressure room if available)
- N95 masks for healthcare workers
- Patient: surgical mask when leaving isolation
- Three consecutive negative sputum smears required before de-isolation

Public Health Obligations:
1. Report to local/national health authority immediately
2. Contact investigation: household contacts and close contacts ≥ 8 hours
3. IGRA/TST for contacts; treat LTBI if positive
4. Social support: housing, nutrition, adherence support

Latent TB Infection (LTBI) Treatment:
- Isoniazid 300mg daily × 9 months
- Rifampicin daily × 4 months (preferred — shorter, less hepatotoxic)
- Isoniazid + Rifapentine weekly × 3 months (3HP regimen)
