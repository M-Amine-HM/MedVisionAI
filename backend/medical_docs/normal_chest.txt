Normal Chest X-Ray — Reference Guide

A normal chest X-ray serves as an important baseline for comparison and indicates no significant
pulmonary pathology is present.

Normal Radiological Anatomy:
Lungs:
- Clear lung fields bilaterally, no opacification
- Normal vascular markings visible from hilum to periphery
- Costophrenic angles sharp (no effusion)
- Lung apices clear

Heart:
- Cardiothoracic ratio < 0.5 on PA view
- Clear cardiac borders (left: aortic knuckle, pulmonary artery, left ventricle)
- Right heart border: SVC, right atrium

Mediastinum:
- Upper mediastinum: trachea midline
- Hila at normal position (left hilum slightly higher than right)
- No hilar lymphadenopathy

Diaphragm:
- Right hemidiaphragm slightly higher than left (liver)
- Gastric air bubble beneath left hemidiaphragm
- Clear costo- and cardiophrenic angles

Bones and Soft Tissues:
- Symmetric ribs, no fractures or lytic lesions
- Vertebral bodies visible, no obvious collapse
- Soft tissues symmetric

What Normal Findings Mean for the Patient:
- No active pulmonary infection detected
- No evidence of cardiac enlargement
- No pleural effusion or pneumothorax
- No suspicious masses or nodules

Clinical Implications:
A normal chest X-ray in the context of respiratory symptoms should prompt consideration of:
- Upper respiratory tract infection (not visible on X-ray)
- Asthma or COPD (may appear normal between exacerbations)
- Pulmonary embolism (X-ray insensitive — requires CT-PA)
- Early interstitial lung disease (may require HRCT)
- Extrapulmonary causes of dyspnea (cardiac, anemia, anxiety)

Recommendations:
- Routine annual health screening as appropriate for age/risk
- Maintain updated vaccination status (influenza, pneumococcal)
- Lifestyle modifications: smoking cessation, exercise, healthy diet
- Return if symptoms develop or worsen
- Consider CT for high-risk individuals (smokers >30 pack-years, age 50-80 -> low-dose CT screening)
